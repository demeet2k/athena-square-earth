def classify_spin(loop: LoopSpec, U: List[Any], NF, dist, budget: Budget, replay_ptr: str, witness_ptrs: List[str]) -> Outcome:
    r = holonomy_residual(loop, U, NF, dist)
    # budget charging: treat r as commutation residual (kappa) and also drift (beta) if the corridor chooses
    kappa_use = r
    beta_use  = 0.0
    chi_use   = len(U)  # placeholder; real corridors charge replay cost, search, and verification
    if r == 0.0:
        return Outcome("OK", replay_ptr, tuple(witness_ptrs), None, None, None,
                       Budget(kappa_use, beta_use, 0, chi_use), tuple())
    if r <= budget.kappa:
        rl = ResidualLedger(metric_id="METRIC_PINNED", bound=r, scope=(loop.loop_id,),
                            obligations=("PROMOTE_BY_TIGHTENING_OR_REFINEMENT",), replay_ptr=replay_ptr)

    return Outcome("FAIL", replay_ptr, tuple(witness_ptrs), None, None, fw,
                   Budget(kappa_use, beta_use, 0, chi_use), tuple())

❀ Flower — Continuous Spin, Holonomy, and Curvature as the Geometry of Noncommutation
09.F1 — Holonomy from flows and connections
Let (\mathcal{M}) be a smooth state manifold (e.g., (\mathbb{T}^d) or a smooth model space whose discretizations produce the Square carriers). Let (A,B) be (possibly unbounded) generators of flows on (\mathcal{M}), with flow maps
[
\Phi_A^t:=e^{tA},\qquad \Phi_B^t:=e^{tB}.
]
Noncommutation is measured by the Lie bracket ([A,B]), and holonomy is the integrated manifestation of that bracket along loops.

09.F2 — Spin loop as a commutator of exponentials
Define the infinitesimal spin loop at step (\Delta t) by the commutator of the two flows:
[
\boxed{
\mathrm{Spin}_{\Delta t}(A,B)
:=e^{\Delta t A},e^{\Delta t B},e^{-\Delta t A},e^{-\Delta t B}.
}
]
This is a loop in the diffeomorphism group (or semigroup, when only partial inverses exist) acting on (\mathcal{M}).
When the Baker–Campbell–Hausdorff expansion is valid on a corridor domain, the leading-order holonomy is:
[
\boxed{
\mathrm{Spin}_{\Delta t}(A,B)
=\exp!\left(\Delta t^2 [A,B] + O(\Delta t^3)\right).
}
]
Thus, the commutator bracket ([A,B]) is the curvature density controlling spin at small steps.

09.F3 — Holonomy as curvature integrated over a loop
Let a path (\gamma:[0,1]\to\mathcal{M}) be generated by a time-dependent vector field (V(t)). A connection-like transport rule (pinned by corridor policy) defines parallel transport along (\gamma). The holonomy of a closed loop (\gamma) is the induced endomorphism on the fiber (or on the state space when the transport is intrinsic).
Spin receipts in the discrete HD‑SCT graph correspond to sampled holonomy of such loop transports; the pinned commutation budget (\kappa) is a discrete corridor bound on curvature-integral magnitude.

09.F4 — Corridor geometry: spin‑kill and curvature damping
A corridor may enforce a spin-kill constraint: only allow operator schedules whose commutator residual is bounded or whose higher-order symmetric compositions cancel leading curvature terms.
Canonical examples include:
Symmetric splitting schedules that reduce leading-order spin accumulation (e.g., Strang-type symmetrizations), and
Curvature damping rules that refine resolution or change charts when spin receipts exceed (\kappa).
In HD‑SCT, these are realized as route constraints requiring specific SpinReceipts to be (\mathrm{OK}) or (\mathrm{NEAR}) before a composite schedule is admitted as routable.

☁ Cloud — Spin as a Random Variable and Receipts as Confidence-Bearing Evidence
09.C1 — Noisy evaluation and probabilistic holonomy
When measurements, floating representations, or stochastic approximations enter, loop evaluation yields a random residual (\widehat{\mathrm{Res}}\pi(L;U)). Under a pinned noise model, define:
[
\widehat{r}:=\widehat{\mathrm{Res}}\pi(L;U),
\qquad
r:=\mathbb{E}[\widehat{r}],
\qquad
\mathrm{Var}(\widehat{r})\ \text{pinned or estimated}.
]
A corridor must pin the inference rule mapping (\widehat{r}) into truth typing; absent pinned inference, the outcome is (\mathrm{AMBIG}).

09.C2 — Probabilistic receipts: confidence and risk envelopes
A ProbabilisticSpinReceipt extends (\mathsf{SR}) with confidence structure:
estimator specification,
confidence intervals for residual,
sample size and sampling plan digest,
failure probabilities or tail bounds,
replay recipe reproducing the estimator and randomness control (seed pinning or distribution pinning).
Truth typing rules remain discrete:
(\mathrm{OK}) requires deterministic zero residual under pinned exactness (or a pinned probabilistic notion of “almost surely zero” that is itself replayable and publishable),
(\mathrm{NEAR}) requires bounded residual with disclosed confidence and scope,
(\mathrm{AMBIG}) requires a bounded candidate set (e.g., multiple plausible holonomy magnitudes due to insufficient samples) and an evidence plan,
(\mathrm{FAIL}) requires a reproducible violation beyond budgets or a reproducible inability to bound risk under (\chi).

09.C3 — Uncertainty propagation through routes
Given a route (P) composed of edges with outcomes (\tau_i), the route truth is the meet (\bigwedge_i\tau_i). For spin receipts, uncertainty propagates as:
any required commutation receipt typed (\mathrm{FAIL}) blocks the route,
(\mathrm{AMBIG}) receipts prevent semantic rewriting and force evidence planning,
(\mathrm{NEAR}) receipts impose mandatory residual disclosures and downstream obligations.
Thus probabilistic holonomy is routable only to the extent that the corridor allows NEAR and that disclosures are carried.

09.C4 — Statistical discriminators for AMBIG holonomy
When a loop is typed (\mathrm{AMBIG}), the evidence plan typically specifies discriminators such as:
refinement tests (increase (k) or (B_d) and re-evaluate spin),
domain restriction tests (evaluate on a smaller corridor subset),
schedule changes (alternate generator orderings and compare receipts),
cross-lens conjugacy checks (transport loop to a different lens and re-measure under a transport-invariant metric).
All discriminators must be finite and (\chi)-bounded to remain (\mathrm{AMBIG}); otherwise the corridor must type (\mathrm{FAIL}).

✶ Fractal — Spin Accumulation Across Scale and Governance by Receipts
09.R1 — Spin growth and path dependence under repeated composition
Even when each individual step appears corridor-close, repeated noncommutation can accumulate into macroscopic drift. Let (a,b) be two endomorphisms and consider repeated schedules such as ((ab)^n) versus ((ba)^n). The discrepancy is governed by repeated commutator insertions:
[
(ab)^n = (ba)^n \cdot \prod_{j=1}^{n-1} \mathrm{Spin}(a,b)^{\theta_j},
]
for corridor-pinned exponents (\theta_j) determined by group identities on the admissible domain. The essential point is that spin receipts must be treated as first-class scale-governance objects: a small nonzero holonomy can become large after many compositions unless the corridor enforces damping, schedule symmetrization, or refinement.

09.R2 — Holonomy localization and curvature concentration
Spin is not generally uniform across state space. The corridor therefore permits localized receipts:
evaluate on structured test sets (U) (hub meshes, carry hyperplanes, station partitions),
compute scope-limited residual ledgers,
and attach these to routes that traverse the same scope.
This yields a holonomy atlas: a map from operator pairs and scopes to certified receipts. Curvature is treated as concentrated where commutators are large (e.g., on boundary loci such as carry sets, aliasing corridors, or hub transitions), and the atlas supports targeted patching.

09.R3 — Governance: commutation receipts as non-negotiable artifacts
A transport planner is permitted to assert path equivalence only via a holonomy witness:
Two distinct routes are equivalent in a corridor only if the path-difference loop receipt is (\mathrm{OK}) (or (\mathrm{NEAR}) when the corridor permits NEAR rewriting with disclosures and obligations).
Non-adjacent representation swaps or composite lift chains require commutation receipts for the reorderings they implicitly assume.
Any claim of “commutes” without a SpinReceipt (or an explicitly referenced previously certified receipt in the atlas) is typed (\mathrm{AMBIG}) or (\mathrm{FAIL}) according to corridor policy.
Receipts therefore serve as the governance layer that replaces informal “should commute” intuition with certified loop evidence.

09.R4 — Repair loop: spin‑kill, refinement, and migration
When a required SpinReceipt is not (\mathrm{OK}), corridor-governed repair proceeds by the minimal admissible option:
Promotion repair (NEAR→OK): refine resolution, tighten domain, strengthen invariants, or change schedule to reduce residual to zero; reissue receipt with replay closure.
Disambiguation repair (AMBIG): execute evidence plan discriminators to produce a unique candidate receipt and re-evaluate.
Meaning evolution (MIGRATE): if the only way to reconcile is to change pinned profiles (normal forms, metrics, operator definitions), produce an explicit migration with compatibility matrix and regressions; receipts bind to the migrated profiles.
Quarantine (FAIL): if residual violates budgets or replay fails, fence routes that depend on the failed commutation and store a failure witness; future routing must avoid the fenced region unless repaired or migrated.
This closes the holonomy governance loop: spin is measured, typed, routed, and either controlled or fenced.

Ch10 — Meta-Zero and Snap
Alternating projections → canonical representative + residual ledger
Let (\mathsf X) be a complete inner-product state space (finite-dimensional (\mathbb{R}^n), (\mathbb{C}^n), or a separable Hilbert space) equipped with norm (|\cdot|). Let (\mathfrak G) be the directed multigraph of representation changes available inside HD‑SCT at fixed ((d,k)) (square/circle/triangle carriers at a locked depth), and let each oriented edge (e: X \to Y) be equipped with a forward map (f_e: X\to Y) and a backward map (g_e: Y\to X) (possibly approximate or corridor‑restricted).
10.0 Global primitives (used by all lenses)
10.0.1 Zero corridors (per pair)
For any pair ((X\leftrightarrow Y)) with forward (f:X\to Y) and backward (g:Y\to X), define the zero corridor on (X) as the fixed‑set of the round‑trip map:
[
Z_X ;:=; \mathrm{Fix}(g\circ f);=;{x\in X:; g(f(x))=x}.
]
Equivalently, (Z_Y:=\mathrm{Fix}(f\circ g)) on (Y). These sets are the maximal corridors on which the “becoming” (X\to Y\to X) is lossless (exactly reversible).
For any (x\in X), define its distance‑to‑zero residual (a corridor‑exit witness):
[
r_{(f,g)}(x);:=;\bigl|x-(g\circ f)(x)\bigr|.
]
A zero corridor certificate is a bound (r_{(f,g)}(x)\le \beta_{(f,g)}) under a pinned metric; exceeding the bound is corridor violation.
10.0.2 The meta‑zero intersection
HD‑SCT carries multiple logically independent corridor families. The core meta‑zero (the “zero point of everything”) is the intersection of the principal corridor families:
[
\mathcal Z_\star;:=; Z_{\text{carrier}}\cap Z_{\text{Nyquist}}\cap Z_{\Pi}\cap Z_{\text{spec}}\cap Z_{\text{spin}}.
]
On (\mathcal Z_\star), all intended rotations commute (up to the chosen tolerances), and all cross‑lens transports become genuine changes of coordinates rather than lossy reinterpretations.
10.0.3 Corridor projectors and fixed‑set enforcement
A corridor projector is any deterministic map (\mathcal P:\mathsf X\to\mathsf X) such that (\mathrm{Fix}(\mathcal P)=Z) for a corridor set (Z\subseteq\mathsf X), together with a residual functional (r_{\mathcal P}(x):=|x-\mathcal P(x)|) (or a pinned equivalent).
Key corridor‑enforcement gates used throughout the framework include:
Bandlimit / alias‑kill gate: (\mathcal P_B).
Representability gate: (\Pi_h = R_h S_h).
Low‑band “same‑physics” spectral gate: (P_{\Lambda}) (or (P_{\le\Lambda})).
Spin‑kill / commutator‑damping gate: (\mathcal P_{\text{spin},\varepsilon}), projecting near (\ker([D,\Omega])) (or the appropriate holonomy‑null corridor in HD‑SCT).
These gates need not commute. The point of this chapter is to canonically reconcile them.
10.0.4 The Snap operator (canonical corridor lock)
Fix a deterministic ordered gate list (\mathbf P=(P_1,\dots,P_m)), with each (P_i) enforcing one corridor family. Define the one‑step composite:
[
T ;:=; P_m\circ\cdots\circ P_1,
]
and define the Snap (corridor‑lock) output as the fixed‑point of alternating projections (when the limit exists):
[
\psi_\star ;:=; \lim_{n\to\infty}T^n(\psi).
]
A canonical Snap engine returns (\psi_\star) and a structured residual ledger containing: path defects (|\Delta|), alias leakage, rank/conditioning witnesses, and spin magnitude (plus any additional pinned residual metrics mandated by corridor policy).

10.1.2 Rotations: corridor‑lock as a deterministic projector stack
A square‑compatible Snap engine is built as a projector stack (an operator registry plus a deterministic script), with ordering treated as a first‑class, pinned parameter because noncommuting gates imply order sensitivity.

Rollback and re‑lock are defined as first‑class protocols in the snap script (not ad‑hoc interventions).

Because gates do not perfectly commute, the Snap engine must quantify which corridor(s) are failing and by how much. The square lens therefore requires a residual ledger per projector, plus blame attribution keyed to corridor families (representability, rank stability, commutation, etc.).

Required square residual channels (minimum).

10.1.4 Patches: corridor tightening, rollback, and empty‑intersection recovery
Square patching is policy‑driven:
Corridor tightening/loosening rules.
Each corridor family carries threshold selection rules: tighten when residuals indicate structural drift; loosen only if the corridor profile explicitly permits widening (and such widening must trigger MIGRATE if it changes certified semantics).
Deterministic snap scripts.

Intersection feasibility and fallback.
Meta‑zero is an intersection corridor. If the intersection is empty (or empirically infeasible under pinned budgets), the engine must:

Replay harness and transcript discipline.
All corridor evaluations must be reproducible under replay; transcripts record corridor choice, pins, witnesses, residuals, budgets, truth outcome (\tau), and obligations/fences, and are append‑only. Replay capsules reproduce the entire evaluation deterministically under pins.

A hierarchical (fractal) hub mesh may be indexed by octave/level (n), with refinement (\mathcal H_n\subset\mathbb T^d) growing by depth; multi‑step convergence occurs when hub sets are hierarchical rather than coarse. 10.3 △ Triangle lens
10.3.1 Atoms: corridor policy as a projector‑selectable constraint
Triangle is the corridor‑policy layer: it selects which corridors are pinned as admissible routes (global vs per‑axis pinning, and how “visibility corridors” are interpreted). In Snap terms, triangle contributes:
a discrete policy state (\tau) (global (\in{A,B}) or per‑axis (\in{A,B}^d)), and
a policy‑constraint set (Z_\triangle(\tau)\subseteq\mathsf X) defining which transports and which gate parameters are legal.
Triangle’s meta‑zero contribution is the intersection of “policy‑consistent” corridors:
[
Z_{\triangle} ;:=; \bigcap_{\text{declared policy constraints}} Z_{\triangle,i}(\tau).
]
This is enforced by a deterministic policy gate (P_\triangle) that updates or validates (\tau) under pinned tie‑break.
10.3.2 Rotations: policy snap as constrained minimization with deterministic tie‑break
Triangle’s gate is not a metric projection in a continuous space; it is a selection operator over a finite policy set.
Given a candidate state (\psi), define a pinned objective (policy feasibility + residual cost):
[
\mathcal J_\triangle(\tau;\psi);:=;\sum_{i\in\mathcal I_\triangle} w_i, r_i!\bigl(P_i^{(\tau)}(\psi)\bigr)^2
;+;\sum_{b\in\mathcal B_\triangle} \mathbf 1{\text{budget }b\text{ violated under }\tau},
]
where (P_i^{(\tau)}) are the gates whose parameters depend on (\tau), and (\mathcal B_\triangle) are pinned corridor budgets. Triangle snap chooses:
[
\tau_\star ;:=; \operatorname*{argmin}{\tau\in\mathcal T{\text{pinned}}}\mathcal J_\triangle(\tau;\psi),
]
breaking ties by a pinned total order on (\mathcal T_{\text{pinned}}) (so the route is replayable and verifier‑consistent). Any adaptive choice that changes decode‑relevant behavior must be encoded/replayable; otherwise it is illegal.
The triangle output is ((\tau_\star,\psi)) plus (i) the policy regret gap (difference to second best), and (ii) a scope‑bound statement of which transports were excluded by the policy.

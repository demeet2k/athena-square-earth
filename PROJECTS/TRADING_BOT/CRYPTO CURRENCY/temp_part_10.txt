PART III: VEDIC/HINDU CYCLES — THE YUGA SYSTEM
The Vedic system presents the most elaborate fractal time structure, with cycles nested from the breath of Brahma down to the blinking of an eye. This is not mythology in the dismissive sense — it is a precise mathematical cosmology.
A. THE FOUR YUGAS — CONSCIOUSNESS DESCENDING THROUGH ELEMENTS
The 4:3:2:1 Ratio
The Yugas follow the ratio 4:3:2:1, which is not arbitrary. It represents consciousness "stepping down" through the four elemental states:
Total: 4 + 3 + 2 + 1 = 10 = Tetraktys = Sefirot = Complete manifest number
The Four Yugas in Detail
SATYA YUGA (Golden Age) — The Age of Truth
Characteristics of Satya Yuga:
Humans live in direct communion with the divine
No need for religion because truth is self-evident
No disease, no decay, no scarcity
Humans are naturally virtuous
Thought and manifestation are nearly instantaneous
There is no distinction between "spiritual" and "material" — all is spirit
The Bull of Dharma (Cosmic Law):
Dharma is symbolized as a bull standing on four legs:
In Satya Yuga: All four legs (Truth, Austerity, Compassion, Cleanliness) are intact
In each successive Yuga, one leg is lost
TRETA YUGA (Silver Age) — The Age of Ritual
Characteristics of Treta Yuga:
Virtue still predominates but requires effort
Rituals and sacrifices become necessary to maintain cosmic order
Kings emerge to govern (necessary because self-governance weakens)
The first wars occur
Sacred knowledge must be preserved in texts (Vedas compiled)
Agriculture begins (direct provision from nature weakens)
The Loss of Truth: The leg of Truth (Satya) weakens. Not that people lie more — rather, truth becomes less self-evident. It must be sought, argued, proven.
The Ramayana Epic: The story of Rama occurs in Treta Yuga. Rama embodies dharmic kingship — ruling according to cosmic law even at great personal cost.
DVAPARA YUGA (Bronze Age) — The Age of Duality
Characteristics of Dvapara Yuga:
Virtue and vice are in balance — genuine moral struggle
Duality becomes the dominant experience (us/them, good/bad)
Devotion (bhakti) becomes the path because direct knowing is rare
Priestly class gains power (intermediaries needed)
Disease, shortened lifespan, and conflict increase
Sacred knowledge becomes restricted (Vedas "closed")
The Loss of Austerity: The leg of Austerity (Tapas) weakens. Self-discipline becomes difficult. External rules and enforcement become necessary.
The Mahabharata Epic: The great war of the Mahabharata occurs at the Dvapara-Kali transition. Krishna delivers the Bhagavad Gita to Arjuna on the battlefield — the ultimate teaching for the coming dark age.
Krishna's Death Marks the Beginning of Kali Yuga: Traditional date: February 17-18, 3102 BCE
KALI YUGA (Iron Age) — The Age of Strife
Characteristics of Kali Yuga:
From Srimad Bhagavatam, Canto 12, Chapter 2:
The One Remaining Leg: Only Cleanliness (Shaucha) remains, and it is weakening. In some interpretations, only the practice of charity (dana) and name repetition (nama-japa) remain effective spiritual paths in Kali Yuga.
The Hidden Blessing of Kali Yuga:
From Srimad Bhagavatam 12.3.51-52:
"My dear King, although Kali-yuga is an ocean of faults, there is still one good quality about this age: Simply by chanting the names of Krishna, one can become free from material bondage and be promoted to the transcendental kingdom.
Whatever result was obtained in Satya-yuga by meditating on Vishnu, in Treta-yuga by performing sacrifices, and in Dvapara-yuga by serving the Lord's lotus feet can be obtained in Kali-yuga simply by chanting the names of Lord Hari."
In other words: what took lifetimes of effort in previous ages can be achieved quickly in Kali Yuga through devotion. The "discount rate" on spiritual progress is highest in the darkest age.

Dawn = transition into the Yuga's quality Dusk = transition out of the Yuga's quality

## What This Does NOT Prove

1. **That VML's semantic interpretations are all correct.** Internal consistency proves coherence, not truth. A well-constructed interpretive system will always be internally consistent. The chemistry matches and historical alignments elevate VML far above "mere coherence," but some semantic assignments may be wrong at the morpheme level while the overall framework is sound.

2. **That the Voynich Manuscript IS a pharmaceutical manual.** VML provides one interpretive framework that works across multiple validation axes. We have not proven that no other framework could also work (though none has been demonstrated). The manuscript has not been "decoded" in the conventional sense — VML provides a consistent reading, not a translation.

3. **That all plant identifications are correct.** Pharmacological claims rest on botanical identifications that are not independently verifiable from the manuscript alone. Where multiple scholarly IDs exist (e.g., f1r: Hellebore vs. Cloves), VML's chemistry match provides discriminating evidence — but this evidence is only as strong as VML itself.

4. **That the system was ever practiced.** Internal consistency proves COHERENCE, not USE. The manuscript could be a theoretical treatise, a teaching text, a personal reference, or a working manual. We cannot distinguish between these from internal evidence alone.

5. **That specific morpheme-to-operation mappings are precisely correct.** We can verify that high o- correlates with heat-requiring plants, but we cannot independently confirm that `otchor` specifically means "heat the energy source at the volatile outlet" as opposed to some other heat-related operation. The granularity of semantic precision remains uncertain.

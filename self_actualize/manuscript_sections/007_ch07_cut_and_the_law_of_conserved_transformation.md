## 7.1 Square - Core Conserved Quantities, Cost Functions, Transformation Legality, and Local/Global Energy Books

### 7.1.1 Core conserved quantities

The first task of CUT is to define what is conserved. The strongest CUT formulation answers this by positing a conserved computational quantity, `\kappa`, which functions as the ledger substance from which more local conserved quantities are derived by projection, coarse-graining, and domain-specific dial choice. Not every domain will observe the same vocabulary, but every lawful domain must be representable as a structured redistribution of `\kappa`.

This means local conserved families such as energy, probability, charge, memory capacity, informational burden, or organizational resource are not treated as unrelated primitives. They are typed views of one deeper bookkeeping substrate. Write the decomposed ledger as

$$
\mathbb{K}(x) = (\kappa_1(x),\dots,\kappa_k(x)),
$$

with total conserved carrier

$$
\kappa_{\mathrm{tot}} = \sum_{j=1}^{k} \kappa_j.
$$

In closed systems, the current `J^\mu_\kappa` satisfies the conservation law

$$
\nabla_\mu J^\mu_\kappa = 0.
$$

In open systems, a source term must be declared rather than silently assumed:

$$
\nabla_\mu J^\mu_\kappa = S_\kappa.
$$

The chapter's use of conserved transformation therefore does not mean nothing changes. It means no lawful change is unaccounted. Substance may move across scale, channel, carrier, or form, but if the transform is lawful, the ledger remains intelligible.

At the level of a local CUT state, the canonical carrier is

$$
\mathbf{X}(t) = \big(\kappa(t), \ell(t), b(t), \Theta(t)\big),
$$

where `\ell(t)` is a regime-ladder index, `b(t) \in \{0,1\}^8` is a control register selecting active mode families, and `\Theta(t)` gathers domain-specific auxiliary coordinates. Transformation is therefore not tracked only by continuous density; it is also tracked by discrete stage and control configuration.

### 7.1.2 Cost functions

The second Square problem is cost. CUT is explicit that no transformation theory is complete unless it states what a transform pays in order to achieve what it gains. The universal information-cost action is written as

$$
\mathcal{A}_I[P] = \int_{\mathcal{T}} \bigl(K_I[P(t)] - I_I[P(t)]\bigr)\,dt,
$$

where `K_I` is computational cost and `I_I` is informational benefit. This is one of the chapter's defining moves: lawful trajectories are no longer unexplained primitives, but extremal solutions of one constrained optimization process.

Define the cost family

$$
\mathcal{K}_{\mathrm{cost}} =
\{
K_{\mathrm{energy}},
K_{\mathrm{time}},
K_{\mathrm{memory}},
K_{\mathrm{representation}},
K_{\mathrm{curvature}},
K_{\mathrm{coordination}},
K_{\mathrm{drift}}
\}
$$

and the benefit family

$$
\mathcal{B}_{\mathrm{gain}} =
\{
B_{\mathrm{predictive}},
B_{\mathrm{compressive}},
B_{\mathrm{controllable}},
B_{\mathrm{replayable}},
B_{\mathrm{integrative}},
B_{\mathrm{generative}}
\}.
$$

For any concrete transform `T`,

$$
K_I[T] = \sum_i \alpha_i K_i(T),
\qquad
I_I[T] = \sum_j \beta_j B_j(T).
$$

No transformation is judged by output alone. It is judged by output relative to explicit cost and by whether that cost stays visible under replay.

### 7.1.3 Transformation legality

The third Square problem is legality. Not every mathematically describable state transition is admissible inside the manuscript. A transform is lawful only when it preserves the required ledger, remains inside the active corridor, and produces typed outcomes compatible with replay and downstream integration.

Let `T : X \to Y`. Then

$$
\mathsf{Lawful}(T)
\iff
\mathsf{LedgerPreserved}(T)
\land
\mathsf{CorridorAdmissible}(T)
\land
\mathsf{TypedOutcome}(T)
\land
\bigl(\mathsf{Replayable}(T) \lor \mathsf{AuditablyDowngraded}(T)\bigr).
$$

This legality law formalizes a recurring CUT design principle: no silent fixes, no silent equivalences, no silent progress. If exact replay is unavailable, the transform must degrade honestly rather than pretending full closure.

### 7.1.4 Local and global energy books

The final Square atom is the distinction between local and global books. A transformation may appear beneficial in one region while violating conservation at the system level, or may improve the global regime while being excessively destructive locally. CUT therefore requires dual bookkeeping.

Define the local ledger for a region `R` as

$$
\kappa(R,t) = \int_R \rho_\kappa(z,t)\, d\Gamma(z)
$$

and the global ledger as

$$
\kappa_{\mathrm{tot}}(t) = \int_\Gamma \rho_\kappa(z,t)\, d\Gamma(z).
$$

The local/global book law is:

$$
\Delta \kappa_{\mathrm{local}} \neq 0
\quad \text{is lawful only if} \quad
\Delta \kappa_{\mathrm{global}} = 0
$$

for closed systems, or only if explicit source and sink terms are declared for open systems.

This distinction is indispensable because later manuscript structures will repeatedly create tension between local repair and global integrity. CUT prevents those tensions from being disguised as isolated wins.

## 7.2 Flower - Dual Transformation Paths, Efficiency Symmetries, Reversible/Irreversible Flows, and Coupled Dynamics

### 7.2.1 Dual transformation paths

The first Flower atom is dual transformation paths. CUT must model not one privileged route of change, but multiple lawful paths through which the same macroscopic objective may be reached. The corpus's use of inversion, rotation, and transport already prepares this move: the same destination may be reachable through different intermediate ledgers, different burden profiles, and different reversibility classes.

Let `P_1` and `P_2` be admissible paths between `X` and `Y`. They are dual if

$$
\mathsf{Goal}(P_1)=\mathsf{Goal}(P_2)=Y
$$

while differing in:

- cost distribution
- intermediate ledger states
- reversibility class
- sensitivity to noise
- cross-scale coupling burden

Their comparison functional is

$$
\Delta_{12}
=
\bigl(
K_I[P_1]-K_I[P_2],\
I_I[P_1]-I_I[P_2],\
\mu[P_1]-\mu[P_2]
\bigr).
$$

Dual routes are therefore first-class dynamic objects, not after-the-fact commentary. The chapter needs them because a lawful system must be able to say not only whether a route exists, but why one admissible route dominates another.

### 7.2.2 Efficiency symmetries

The second Flower atom is efficiency symmetry. At each scale `s`, define the efficiency functional

$$
E_s(C) = \frac{B_s(C)}{K_s(C)},
$$

and the multi-scale efficiency

$$
E_{\mathrm{total}}(C) = \prod_{s \in S} E_s(C)^{w_s},
$$

with scale weights `w_s`.

An efficiency symmetry arises when two apparently distinct transforms have equivalent or predictably related efficiency profiles across the active scale family:

$$
T_1 \sim_E T_2
\iff
\forall s \in S,\ E_s(T_1) \approx E_s(T_2)
$$

within corridor tolerance.

The importance of this law is practical. It tells the Orchestrator when two different implementations are genuinely different and when they are only stylistic variants of one conserved efficiency class.

### 7.2.3 Reversible versus irreversible flows

The third Flower atom distinguishes reversible and irreversible transformation. The CUT corpus already differentiates exact transport, bounded drift, corridor-lossy reduction, and replay-only trace. Chapter 7 turns that distinction into a formal typing rule.

Define

$$
\mathsf{Rev}(T)=1
\iff
\exists T^{-1}\ \text{lawful within the declared corridor},
$$

and

$$
\mathsf{Irr}(T)=1
\iff
\neg \mathsf{Rev}(T).
$$

The stronger subtype family is:

- reversible
- boundedly reversible
- irreversible but replay-auditable
- irreversible and lossy

This distinction matters because irreversible transforms may still be lawful, but only if their lost structure, uncertainty, and replay surface remain typed together rather than being erased rhetorically.

### 7.2.4 Coupled dynamics

The fourth Flower atom is coupled dynamics. Domains do not evolve independently. Local state changes alter edge terms, shared budgets, and higher-order couplings. CUT therefore treats transform law as graph-sensitive rather than atomically local.

Let

$$
\mathcal{S}_G = \prod_{v \in V} \mathcal{S}_v
$$

be a coupled system over graph `G=(V,E)`, with total ledger

$$
\kappa(x)=\sum_{v\in V}\kappa_v(x_v)+\sum_{(u,v)\in E}\kappa_{uv}(x_u,x_v)+\cdots.
$$

A transform is coupled if it changes not only a local subledger but also edge or higher-order coupling terms. This is the mathematical root of later manuscript claims about route bundles, merge debt, trust transport, and deployment spillover. CUT introduces that law here before those later domain-specific manifestations appear.

## 7.3 Cloud - Loss Accounting, Noise Budgets, Hidden Cost Exposure, and Uncertainty Under Transformation

### 7.3.1 Loss accounting

The first Cloud atom is loss accounting. CUT does not allow elegant transformation narratives that hide what was discarded, approximated, or pushed outside the active model. Every transform must therefore emit an explicit loss receipt.

Define the receipt

$$
\Lambda_T =
(\Delta_{\mathrm{gain}},\Delta_{\mathrm{loss}},\Delta_{\mathrm{hold}},\mu_T,\ell_T),
$$

where:

- `\Delta_{\mathrm{gain}}` is stabilized useful structure
- `\Delta_{\mathrm{loss}}` is what was destroyed or compressed away
- `\Delta_{\mathrm{hold}}` is what remains deferred, buffered, or not yet resolved
- `\mu_T` is uncertainty over these quantities
- `\ell_T` is lineage

This object is not optional. Without it, local apparent improvements cannot be judged honestly under later replay or repair.

### 7.3.2 Noise budgets

The second Cloud atom is noise budget. The CUT framework recognizes that different domains treat randomness and uncertainty differently, and that this heterogeneity becomes dangerous unless translated into one ledger-aware language.

Define the noise budget

$$
N_T = (\eta_{\mathrm{obs}}, \eta_{\mathrm{proc}}, \eta_{\mathrm{repr}}, \eta_{\mathrm{ctrl}}),
$$

where the components represent:

- observational noise
- processing noise
- representational noise
- control or update noise

A transform remains corridor-admissible only if

$$
N_T \preceq N_T^{\max}
$$

for its burden class.

Noise is therefore not an embarrassment to hide. It is part of the legality surface of transformation itself.

### 7.3.3 Hidden cost exposure

The third Cloud atom is hidden cost exposure. Some transformations appear cheap only because their costs are displaced:

- into future repair
- into replay debt
- into downstream complexity
- into benchmark drift
- into trust degradation
- into human interpretive overhead

Define hidden cost by

$$
K_{\mathrm{hidden}}(T)=
K_{\mathrm{future}} + K_{\mathrm{repair}} + K_{\mathrm{ambiguity}} + K_{\mathrm{trust}}.
$$

Then the true cost is

$$
K_{\mathrm{true}}(T)=K_{\mathrm{visible}}(T)+K_{\mathrm{hidden}}(T).
$$

This is one of the chapter's most practically important laws, because many harmful optimizations arise from pretending that delayed or displaced burdens are not costs at all.

### 7.3.4 Uncertainty under transformation

The fourth Cloud atom is uncertainty under transformation. A lawful transform carries not only cost and ledger data, but uncertainty about whether it preserved what it claims to preserve, whether its cost estimate is complete, and whether its replay surface is adequate.

Define the transform-uncertainty profile

$$
\mu_T = (\mu_{\mathrm{input}},\mu_{\mathrm{operator}},\mu_{\mathrm{cost}},\mu_{\mathrm{output}},\mu_{\mathrm{replay}}).
$$

The uncertainty law is then:

$$
\mathsf{Completion}(T)\not\Rightarrow \mu_T=0.
$$

Completion is not exactness. A transform may terminate successfully while still carrying ambiguity, approximation, or unverified preservation claims. This law keeps CUT aligned with the manuscript's broader truth lattice and prevents the chapter from collapsing into naive determinist rhetoric.

## 7.4 Fractal - Cross-Scale Conservation, Recursive Optimization, Emergence Cost Stacking, and CUT as Stabilizer of Recursive Growth

### 7.4.1 Cross-scale conservation

The first Fractal atom is cross-scale conservation. One of the recurring failures of domain-specific law is the inability to state how local improvements relate to distant costs or reorganizations. CUT repairs this by giving one conserved ledger and one multi-scale efficiency framework.

Let `S=\{s_1,\dots,s_n\}` be the active scale family. Then cross-scale conservation requires

$$
\sum_{s \in S} \Delta \kappa_s = 0
$$

for closed systems, or that all nonzero residual be explicitly accounted for by reservoir terms in open systems.

This is stronger than local conservation because it forces the system to expose how improvement in one scale neighborhood is paid for elsewhere.

### 7.4.2 Recursive optimization

The second Fractal atom is recursive optimization. Since transformations occur repeatedly, the action functional and efficiency laws are not applied once, but at every level and every cycle.

Define recursive optimization over a transform sequence `(T_1,\dots,T_n)` by

$$
\mathcal{O}_{\mathrm{rec}}
=
\operatorname*{arg\,ext}_{(T_1,\dots,T_n)}
\sum_{i=1}^{n}\bigl(I_I[T_i]-K_I[T_i]\bigr),
$$

subject to:

- ledger preservation
- corridor admissibility
- replay sufficiency
- truth-class transparency

This is what later allows CUT to support manuscript growth, bridge theory, deployment, and compiler behavior without changing its core law.

### 7.4.3 Emergence cost stacking

The third Fractal atom is emergence cost stacking. New layers of organization are not free. If a transform creates a new regime, layer, or manuscript-scale behavior, the costs of all enabling transitions accumulate.

Define emergence stack cost by

$$
K_{\mathrm{stack}}
=
\sum_{i=1}^{n} K_I[T_i]
+
\sum_{i<j} K_{\mathrm{couple}}(T_i,T_j),
$$

where the second term captures interaction costs between layers of emergence.

This law is what later makes it possible to distinguish genuinely efficient recursive growth from fragile escalation. Emergence is not celebrated because it is large. It is judged by whether its total stack remains legal, replayable, and cross-scale coherent.

### 7.4.4 CUT as stabilizer of recursive growth

The final Fractal atom is CUT as stabilizer of recursive growth. CUT is not just a conservation theory. It is the stabilizing grammar by which recursive manuscript growth remains honest. It does so by forcing every growth step to answer:

CUT stabilizes recursion precisely because it refuses free-emergence myths. It turns growth from a rhetorical promise into an accountable chain of conserved, costed, uncertainty-bearing transitions.
